Political Economy of Third World

(Left): A map showing the third world countries. (Middle): A picture of a person harvesting an agricultural farm. (Right): A picture of lion. Copyright ©

This course was about an analysis of political and economic issues in the Third World with special emphasis on economic growth, underdevelopment
and strategies for development. The evolution of capitalism and different roles and functions assigned to all Third Worlds on the periphery as well as the center.
The class paid a special attention to colonialism, nationalism, the international system, state building, and political change, rural and gender perspectives.

Further Description of the Course

The class further analysed a wide variety of themes, from rural and urban poverty to environmental and cultural issues.
It included case studies of organizations that have been influenced by current neoliberal policies; poverty reduction policies and strategies,
critical perspectives on economic restructuring, interrelationships between trade, and economic development; and modernization.
The class also further explained why neoliberalism has become a predominant policy in the various Third World regions in the recent past.
Analyses of the environment, women's issues, rural development and globalization were also covered in this class.

The Topics Covered

The course covered different topics about the economic and political conditions of the Third World/Global South countries.
Some of the topics covered include:

  • The Views of Political Economy
  • Capitalism
  • Communism/Socialism
  • Mercantile System
  • Marxism

The class also covered how the World Bank and the IMF have contributed to the poverty of the Third World Countries.
Different documentaries were shown in class to illustrate how some Third World nations have been affected by the structural adjustments
imposed by the Bank and IMF when these countries want to borrow money from the two financial institutions.

Pictures of Walt Whitman Rostow. Copyright©


This class also covered Rostow's stages of growth which include:
  1. Traditional Society
  2. Preconditions for take-off
  3. Take-off
  4. Drive to Maturity
  5. Age of High Mass Consumption

Kenya's Economy

(Left): Kenyan Map with the national flag; (Middle): Tea Farmers at work; (Right): Nairobi (Capital of Kenya). Copyright©

My research project was about the effect of knowledge, technological innovation, free market and government policies in the Kenyan Economy.
I particularly paid a special attention on how the mobile banking (M-Banking) populary known as M-Pesa has contributed to the country's GDP.
As an economic driving engine in East Africa, Kenya's free market has contributed in increasing the competition between various private businesses which in turn
benefits both the business owners and the consumers. As a result of competition, quality products are produced, prices adjust to market price, and innovations increase.


Konza Technology City

The picture of the Konza Techno City. Copyright©

My project further explained how the Konza techno-city project underlined in Kenya's vision 2030 is going to be a great economic driver for the country if achieved.
The project has been dubbed where African silicon savannah begins and the park is set to host business process outsourcing (BPO) ventures, a science park,
a convention centre, shopping malls, hotels, international schools,and health facility project. The city will be located in Makueni County, 64 km south of Nairobi in 5000 acres of land.
